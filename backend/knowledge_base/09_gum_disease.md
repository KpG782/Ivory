# Gum Disease — Gingivitis and Periodontitis

Gum (periodontal) disease is an infection of the tissues that hold your teeth in place. It is very common — CDC data indicate that nearly half of US adults aged 30 and older show some form of periodontal disease, and it becomes more common with age. It is also largely preventable, and in its early stage fully reversible. This summary is based on NIDCR and CDC public health guidance.

## The two stages

### Gingivitis — early and reversible

When plaque and its hardened form, tartar, sit along the gumline, the bacteria in them inflame the gums. Inflamed gums become red, swollen, and quick to bleed — classically when brushing or flossing. This stage is called **gingivitis**. There is no bone or tissue loss yet, and it can usually be reversed with daily brushing and flossing plus a professional cleaning to remove the tartar you cannot remove at home.

Bleeding gums are not normal and are not a reason to stop brushing — they are the earliest, most fixable warning sign.

### Periodontitis — advanced and managed, not cured

If gingivitis is not addressed, the gums can begin to pull away from the teeth, forming **pockets** that trap plaque below the gumline where a toothbrush cannot reach. The body's immune response to the infection, together with bacterial toxins, starts breaking down the bone and connective tissue that anchor the teeth. This is **periodontitis**. Over time teeth can loosen, drift, and eventually need removal. Bone lost to periodontitis does not grow back on its own, which is why catching gum disease early matters so much.

## Warning signs

- Gums that bleed when you brush or floss
- Red, swollen, or tender gums
- Persistent bad breath or a bad taste that will not go away
- Gums pulling away from the teeth (teeth look "longer")
- Pain when chewing, or new sensitivity near the gumline
- Loose or shifting teeth, or a change in how your bite fits together

Periodontitis can progress with surprisingly little pain — many people first notice it as bad breath or a loose tooth.

## What raises your risk

- **Smoking and tobacco use** — the most significant modifiable risk factor; it also makes treatment less effective.
- **Diabetes** — gum disease and blood sugar control affect each other in both directions.
- Certain medications that reduce saliva flow.
- Hormonal changes, which can make gums more sensitive.
- Genetic susceptibility — some people are simply more prone.
- Poor brushing and flossing habits, which let plaque mature into tartar.

## How gum disease is treated at Ivory Dental Studio

Treatment is matched to the stage:

1. **Gingivitis:** a professional cleaning plus a tuned-up home-care routine usually resolves it. We re-check at your next visit.
2. **Early periodontitis:** a deep cleaning called **scaling and root planing** — removing tartar from below the gumline and smoothing the root surfaces so gums can reattach. This is done comfortably with local numbing, often across two visits.
3. **Ongoing management:** after deep cleaning, most periodontitis patients move to more frequent maintenance cleanings (often every three to four months) to keep pockets from re-infecting.
4. **Advanced cases:** when pockets are too deep for cleaning alone, we discuss antimicrobial treatment and refer to a periodontist (gum specialist) for surgical options such as flap procedures or grafting.

## Prevention

Brush twice daily with fluoride toothpaste, clean between your teeth every day (that is where gum disease starts), do not smoke, manage diabetes if you have it, and keep regular cleaning visits so tartar never gets a foothold. If your gums bleed, look puffy, or your last cleaning was years ago, Ivory can set up a cleaning visit — the exam includes a full gum check.
