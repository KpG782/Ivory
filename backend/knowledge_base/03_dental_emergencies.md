# Dental Emergencies — First Steps

A dental emergency is any mouth problem involving significant pain, swelling, bleeding, or a damaged tooth that needs prompt attention. Ivory Dental Studio reserves same-day slots for urgent visits Monday through Saturday, 8:00 AM to 6:00 PM, and runs an after-hours emergency line at (415) 555-0114. This page covers safe first steps at home — it is educational information, not a diagnosis.

## Toothache

A toothache that lasts more than a day or two, wakes you at night, or reacts sharply to hot, cold, or sweet things often signals tooth decay that has reached the inner pulp of the tooth, or an infection. Untreated, an infected tooth can develop an abscess. First steps:

- Rinse your mouth with warm water.
- Gently floss around the sore tooth — trapped food is a surprisingly common cause of sudden pain.
- Use a cold compress on the outside of your cheek for swelling.
- Take an over-the-counter pain reliever as directed on the label. Do **not** place aspirin directly against the gums or tooth — it can burn the tissue.
- Book an urgent visit. Pain that goes away on its own does not mean the problem is gone; nerves can die quietly while infection continues.

## Chipped or broken tooth

- Rinse your mouth with warm water and save any tooth fragments in a clean container.
- Apply a cold compress to limit swelling.
- If a sharp edge is cutting your tongue or cheek, cover it temporarily with sugar-free chewing gum or over-the-counter dental wax.
- Even a painless chip should be examined soon — cracks can let bacteria reach the pulp.

## Knocked-out permanent tooth

Minutes matter. A permanent tooth replanted within about 30–60 minutes has the best chance of survival:

1. Pick the tooth up by the crown (the chewing surface). Do not touch the root.
2. If dirty, rinse it gently with water for a few seconds. Do not scrub it or remove any attached tissue.
3. If possible, place it back into its socket and bite gently on gauze or a clean cloth to hold it.
4. If you cannot replant it, keep it moist: in a cup of milk, in saline, or tucked inside your cheek (adults only).
5. Get to a dentist immediately.

Baby teeth are **not** replanted — but a child who knocks out a tooth should still be seen promptly.

## Swelling and possible infection

Swelling of the gums, face, or jaw — especially with a bad taste, fever, or a pimple-like bump on the gum — can indicate an abscess, a pocket of infection that will not resolve on its own. Book an urgent visit the same day. Go to a hospital emergency room instead if you have facial swelling together with fever, difficulty swallowing, difficulty breathing, or swelling that is spreading toward the eye or neck — these can become life-threatening.

## Lost filling or crown

- Keep the crown if you find it; it can often be re-cemented.
- Avoid chewing on that side and avoid very hot, cold, or sweet foods, since the exposed tooth may be sensitive.
- Over-the-counter temporary dental cement from a pharmacy can protect the tooth for a day or two, but it is not a repair.
