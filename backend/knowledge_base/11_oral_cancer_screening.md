# Oral Cancer Screening

Every routine exam at Ivory Dental Studio includes a brief oral cancer screening — a few minutes that can catch a serious disease at its most treatable stage. This page summarizes public health information from the National Institute of Dental and Craniofacial Research (NIDCR) about oral and oropharyngeal cancer: what it is, who is at risk, what the screening involves, and which symptoms should never be ignored.

## What oral cancer is

Oral cancer includes cancers of the lips, tongue, cheeks, floor of the mouth, hard palate, and gums; closely related oropharyngeal cancers arise at the back of the throat, including the base of the tongue and the tonsils. Detected early, these cancers are far easier to treat and outcomes are substantially better — which is exactly why screening is built into routine dental exams rather than saved for when something hurts. Early oral cancer frequently does not hurt.

## Who is at higher risk

Based on NIDCR guidance, the major risk factors are:

- **Tobacco in any form** — cigarettes, cigars, pipes, and smokeless (chewing) tobacco.
- **Heavy alcohol use** — and the combination of tobacco plus heavy alcohol multiplies risk well beyond either alone.
- **HPV infection** — human papillomavirus, particularly HPV-16, is now a leading cause of oropharyngeal (back-of-throat) cancers, including in people who never smoked.
- **Sun exposure** — a risk factor specifically for lip cancer; lip balm with sunscreen helps.
- **Age and sex** — risk rises with age, with most cases found in people over 40, and men are affected more often than women.

Having a risk factor does not mean you will develop cancer — and some people with no known risk factors do — which is why we screen everyone, at every checkup.

## What the screening involves

The screening is quick, painless, and requires no special preparation:

1. The dentist looks carefully at your lips, gums, tongue (top, sides, and underneath), the floor of your mouth, your cheeks, and the roof of your mouth.
2. You will be asked to stick out your tongue and say "ah" so the back of the mouth and throat can be checked.
3. The dentist gently feels along your jaw and neck for lumps or swollen lymph nodes.
4. Anything unusual is measured, noted, and re-checked — or referred for evaluation and possible biopsy if it looks suspicious or has persisted.

## Signs and symptoms to take seriously

See a dentist promptly — do not wait for your next checkup — if you notice any of the following lasting **more than two weeks**:

- A sore, irritation, or ulcer in the mouth or on the lip that does not heal
- A red or white patch on the gums, tongue, or lining of the mouth
- A lump, thickening, rough spot, or crusted or eroded area
- Numbness, pain, or tenderness in the mouth or lips without an obvious cause
- Difficulty chewing, swallowing, speaking, or moving the jaw or tongue
- A feeling of something caught in the throat, or a change in how your teeth fit together

Most sores and patches turn out to be harmless — bite injuries, canker sores, irritation from dentures — but "it went away and came back" or "it's been there a month" is exactly what should be examined.

## Lowering your risk

Do not use tobacco (quitting at any age reduces risk), limit alcohol, protect your lips from the sun, and ask your medical provider about HPV vaccination, which protects against the virus types responsible for most HPV-related oropharyngeal cancers. And keep regular dental checkups — the screening only works if you are in the chair. If it has been a while, Ivory can set up a cleaning visit; the oral cancer screening is part of the exam.
