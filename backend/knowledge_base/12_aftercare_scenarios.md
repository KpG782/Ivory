# Aftercare Scenarios

What to do after common procedures, and when a symptom means you should call us. These are Ivory Dental Studio's standard aftercare instructions, with background drawn from NIDCR and CDC oral health guidance. They are educational, not a diagnosis — for anything alarming, call (415) 555-0100 during business hours or the emergency line at (415) 555-0114 after hours.

## After a tooth extraction

The first 24 hours are about protecting the blood clot that forms in the socket — it is the foundation of healing.

**Do:**

- Bite gently but firmly on the gauze pad for 30–60 minutes after the procedure; replace it if bleeding continues.
- Rest for the remainder of the day and keep your head elevated.
- Use a cold compress on the cheek in 15-minutes-on, 15-minutes-off cycles to limit swelling.
- Eat soft, cool foods (yogurt, smoothies by spoon, mashed potatoes) and chew on the other side.
- Take pain relief as directed. Some oozing and pink-tinged saliva on day one is normal.
- Starting 24 hours after the extraction, rinse gently with warm salt water (half a teaspoon of salt in a cup of warm water) after meals.

**Do not (for at least 24–72 hours):**

- Drink through a straw, spit forcefully, or rinse vigorously — the suction and pressure can dislodge the clot.
- Smoke or use tobacco; it interferes with clotting and healing.
- Poke the site with your tongue, fingers, or toothbrush bristles (brush the rest of your mouth normally).
- Do heavy exercise on day one.

**Call us if:** bleeding will not slow after an hour of firm gauze pressure; swelling or pain gets *worse* on day three or four instead of better; you develop fever or a bad taste with throbbing pain. Worsening pain a few days out can mean **dry socket** — a dislodged clot exposing the bone — which is very treatable, but only if you come in.

## After teeth whitening

- Mild tooth sensitivity and gum irritation for one to three days are the most common side effects and settle on their own.
- Use a fluoride toothpaste made for sensitive teeth for a few days; avoid very hot or icy foods and drinks while sensitivity lasts.
- For the first 48 hours, freshly whitened enamel picks up stains more easily: avoid coffee, tea, red wine, cola, berries, tomato sauce, and tobacco. The informal rule: if it would stain a white shirt, wait.
- Remember that whitening changes natural enamel only — crowns, veneers, and fillings stay their original shade.
- **Call us if:** sensitivity is severe, lasts beyond about a week, or you notice white patches on the gums that do not fade within a day.

## After a filling or crown

- Numbness from local anesthetic lasts a few hours — avoid chewing until it wears off so you do not bite your cheek or tongue.
- Mild sensitivity to cold or pressure for a week or two after a filling is common. A high spot that makes the tooth hit first when you bite is not — call, the adjustment takes five minutes.
- With a temporary crown, avoid sticky or hard foods and floss by sliding the floss *out* sideways rather than snapping it up.

## Tooth sensitivity in general

Sensitivity — a short, sharp response to cold, heat, sweets, or brushing — usually comes from worn enamel or gum recession exposing the softer root surface. Common contributors are brushing too hard, acidic foods and drinks, teeth grinding, and recent dental work. Sensible first steps: switch to a soft-bristled brush and lighter pressure, use a desensitizing fluoride toothpaste consistently for two weeks, and cut down on acidic drinks. Sensitivity that is new, worsening, or focused on one tooth deserves an exam — it can also be the first sign of a cavity or a cracked tooth, and lingering pain (not just a brief zing) suggests the nerve is involved.

## When in doubt

If something feels wrong after any procedure, call. Describing the symptom takes two minutes, and "come in today" versus "that is normal, give it two days" is exactly the judgment call we are here for. Ivory can also set up an urgent visit any time by collecting your name, phone number, problem type, and pain level.
