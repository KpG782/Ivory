# Tooth Decay and Cavities

Tooth decay is damage to a tooth's surface caused by bacteria. It is one of the most common chronic conditions in the United States for both children and adults, and it is largely preventable. This page summarizes public health guidance from the National Institute of Dental and Craniofacial Research (NIDCR) and the CDC on how decay happens, how to spot it, and how it is treated at each stage.

## How decay happens

Your mouth naturally hosts bacteria that collect in plaque, the sticky film that forms on teeth. When you eat or drink foods containing sugars or starches, plaque bacteria use them to produce acids. Those acids attack the tooth's hard outer layer — the enamel — pulling out minerals in a process called demineralization.

Your body fights back: minerals in saliva, and fluoride from toothpaste and drinking water, help redeposit minerals into weakened enamel. Tooth decay is the result of this tug-of-war going the wrong way over time — repeated acid attacks, with too little repair in between, until the enamel loses so much mineral that it breaks down and a cavity (a permanent hole) forms.

This is why *how often* you consume sugar matters as much as how much: sipping soda or snacking through the day keeps teeth under near-constant acid attack, giving saliva no chance to repair the damage.

## Stages and symptoms

- **Early decay:** often has no symptoms at all. A chalky white spot on the enamel can be the first visible sign — at this stage the damage can still be reversed, because enamel can remineralize with fluoride and good home care.
- **Cavity:** once enamel is broken down, the damage is permanent and the tooth needs a filling. You may notice sensitivity to sweet, hot, or cold things, food catching in one spot, or visible brown or black staining.
- **Deep decay:** if untreated, decay advances through the softer dentin layer toward the pulp — the tooth's nerve and blood supply. This tends to cause a genuine toothache, pain when biting, and sensitivity that lingers.
- **Infection:** bacteria reaching the pulp can cause infection and an abscess — a pocket of pus that can produce severe pain, swelling, and fever, and needs prompt treatment.

Because early decay is silent, regular checkups and X-rays are how it gets caught while it is still cheap and easy to handle.

## What raises your risk

- Limited fluoride exposure (non-fluoridated water, not using fluoride toothpaste).
- Frequent sugary or starchy snacks and drinks, including juice and sports drinks.
- Dry mouth — often a side effect of common medications — because saliva is the mouth's built-in repair system.
- Deep grooves in back teeth that trap plaque (sealants help here).
- Receding gums, which expose root surfaces that decay faster than enamel.
- Infrequent brushing and flossing.

## Prevention

- Brush twice a day with a fluoride toothpaste, and clean between teeth daily.
- Drink fluoridated tap water — community water fluoridation is one of the most effective population-wide protections against cavities.
- Limit sugary drinks and between-meal snacks; if you have them, have them with meals.
- Ask about dental sealants for children's (and sometimes adults') back teeth.
- Keep regular checkup and cleaning appointments so early decay is caught at the reversible stage.

## How we treat decay at Ivory Dental Studio

Treatment depends on the stage: **fluoride treatment** can arrest very early decay; a **filling** repairs a standard cavity; a **crown** restores a tooth too damaged for a filling; **root canal treatment** clears infection when decay has reached the pulp; and **extraction** is the last resort when a tooth cannot be saved.
